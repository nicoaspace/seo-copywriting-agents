# Service Pages

**Stage:** MOFU → BOFU
**Objective:** Convince a prospect who already knows their problem that YOUR service is the right solution. The visitor isn't browsing — they're evaluating. Your job is to make the decision obvious.

---

## Guiding Principle

A service page is not a capabilities list. It's a case for why this service, delivered by this team, is the best path from the visitor's current pain to their desired outcome. Every section should move the reader closer to "these are the people I want to work with."

**The test:** If you swapped out your brand name for a competitor's and the page still made sense, it's too generic. Rewrite it.

**Prose first.** The structure below uses bullets to *describe sections to the writer* — it does not mean the published page should be a wall of bulleted lists. Hero copy, problem section, process steps, "who this is for", and the closing should be written as short paragraphs that sound like a person, not a slide deck. Reserve bullets for places where parallel scanning genuinely helps the reader: the FAB block when comparing features against alternatives, the FAQ Q&A, or a 3-5 step process where each step has a verb-led title plus a sentence of explanation. If a "list" only has 3 items and each is a single line, write it as a sentence instead.

## Recommended Structure

### 1. Hero Section
- **Headline:** The outcome the client wants, not the name of the service.
- **Subheadline:** Who it's for + how you deliver it differently. 1-2 lines.
- **Primary CTA:** "Get a Free Consultation", "Request a Quote", "See How It Works".
- **Quick credential:** Number of clients served, years of experience, or industry specialization.

**Technique:** Lead with the result. "Reduce employee turnover by 40%" beats "HR Consulting Services."

❌ "Our Digital Marketing Services"
✅ "More qualified leads from Google — without wasting budget on ads that don't convert"

❌ "Consulting Services for Businesses"
✅ "We help SaaS companies cut churn in half within 6 months"

### 2. The Problem (empathy section)
- Describe the pain the prospect is experiencing. Use their words, not yours.
- Show that you understand the consequences of NOT solving it.
- 2-3 short paragraphs maximum. Don't lecture — empathize.

**Technique:** PAS (Problem → Agitation → Solution) works perfectly here. Name the problem, agitate the cost of inaction, then bridge to your service.

### 3. The Service — What You Actually Do
- Explain the service clearly in plain language. No jargon overload.
- Use FAB for each core component:
  - **Feature:** What the service includes
  - **Advantage:** Why that matters vs. doing it in-house or with a competitor
  - **Benefit:** The tangible result for the client

- Break into 3-5 pillars or components. Each with a clear heading.
- Format: icon + title + 2-3 line description per pillar.

### 4. How It Works (Process)
- 3-5 steps that demystify the engagement.
- The prospect needs to see the path: what happens after they say yes?
- Use action verbs for each step title.
- Include timelines if possible: "Week 1: Discovery call and audit."

**Why this matters:** Uncertainty about "what happens next" is a major objection for services. Showing a clear process reduces perceived risk.

### 5. Proof — Case Studies / Results
- 1-3 case studies with concrete metrics.
- Structure: Challenge → What We Did → Result (with numbers).
- If you don't have case studies, use specific testimonials with name, role, company, and measurable outcome.

❌ "Great experience, highly recommend."
✅ "They reduced our cost per acquisition from $180 to $62 in Q1. We've been working together for 2 years now." — Sarah K., VP Marketing, TechCorp

### 6. Who This Is For (and Who It's Not For)
- Clearly define the ideal client profile.
- Optional: state who this is NOT for. This builds credibility and filters unqualified leads.
- "This service is built for B2B SaaS companies with $1M+ ARR that have a sales team but no dedicated marketing ops."

### 7. FAQ / Objection Handling
- Answer 4-6 of the most common questions/objections.
- Cover: pricing model, timelines, what they need to provide, minimum commitments, and guarantees.
- Be direct. Vagueness in a service page screams "we'll upsell you later."

### 8. CTA Section
- Clear, specific CTA that matches the service's sales cycle.
- For high-ticket services: "Book a Free Strategy Call" > "Contact Us"
- Include risk-reduction microcopy: "No commitment. 30-minute call. We'll send you a custom plan regardless."
- Add a secondary CTA for those not ready: "Download our [Industry] Guide"

## SEO for Service Pages

Service pages target mid-to-high intent keywords. They're critical for local SEO and industry-specific searches.

- **Target keywords:** "[service] + [location]", "[service] for [industry]", "[service] company/agency", "best [service] for [use case]"
- **Title tag:** `[Service Name] for [Audience] | [Brand]` — max 60 characters
- **H1:** Must contain the primary keyword. Outcome-driven when possible.
- **Meta description:** Keyword + differentiator + CTA. 150-160 characters.
- **URL:** `/services/service-name` — clean, no parameters
- **Internal linking:** Link to related services, case studies, and blog content that supports the service.
- **Schema markup:** Service schema with provider, area served, and service type.
- **Minimum content:** 1,000-2,000 words of substantive text. Thin service pages don't rank.

## Techniques by Situation

| Situation | Recommended technique |
|---|---|
| Commoditized service (many competitors) | Lead with differentiator + social proof in hero |
| High-ticket / complex service | Detailed process section + ROI-focused case studies |
| New/unknown service category | Educate first (what it is + why it matters) before selling |
| Local service | Location-specific language, local case studies, Google Business references |
| B2B service | Logic + ROI + industry-specific proof |
| B2C service | Transformation + before/after + emotional testimonials |

## Anti-patterns to Avoid

- ❌ Listing capabilities without connecting them to client outcomes
- ❌ "We are passionate about [service]" — everyone says this
- ❌ No clear process section (leaves the prospect wondering "but how?")
- ❌ Generic stock photos of handshakes and conference rooms
- ❌ Hiding pricing model entirely (even a range or "starting at" is better than nothing)
- ❌ CTA that says "Contact Us" without specifying what happens next
- ❌ Copying the same template across all service pages with just the service name swapped
- ❌ No proof — no case studies, no testimonials, no metrics
- ❌ **Over-listing.** Turning the problem section, the "who this is for" section, or the process narrative into bullet lists. These work better as 1-3 short paragraphs that show empathy and reasoning.
- ❌ **Decorative pillar bullets.** A grid of 3-5 "pillars" where each one is a single line with no concrete benefit or example.
